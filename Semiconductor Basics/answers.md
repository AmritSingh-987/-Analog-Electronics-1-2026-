Question 1: n-type vs. p-type materials

n-type (Negative): This semiconductor material is doped with elements that have extra electrons (like phosphorus). This creates an abundance of free electrons, making electrons the "majority carriers" of the electrical charge.

p-type (Positive): This material is doped with elements that have fewer electrons (like boron). This creates "holes" (the absence of an electron). In a p-type material, these positive holes act as the "majority carriers."

Question 2: Forward and Reverse Bias

Forward-bias: This happens when you connect the positive terminal of a voltage source to the p-type side and the negative terminal to the n-type side. This pushes the electrons and holes toward the junction in the middle, narrowing the depletion region and allowing a large current to flow easily.

Reverse-bias: This happens when you flip the connections (positive to n-type, negative to p-type). This pulls the electrons and holes away from the junction, widening the depletion region. As a result, it acts like a wall, and almost zero current flows (except for a very tiny leakage current).

Question 3: The "Ideal" Device

In electronics, an "ideal" device is a theoretical model that works perfectly without any real-world flaws or power losses. For example, an ideal diode would act like a perfect switch—having absolute zero resistance when forward-biased (letting infinite current through without dropping any voltage) and infinite resistance when reverse-biased (blocking 100% of the current perfectly).
